German[edit]

\n

Alternative forms[edit]

\n
  • ham (in informal writing)
    • \n
  • han (archaic, dialectal)
\n

Etymology[edit]

\n

From Middle High German haben, from Old High German hab\u0113n (Akin to Old Saxon hebbian, Old Norse hafa (Swedish hava/ha), Old Frisian habba, Gothic \ud800\udf37\ud800\udf30\ud800\udf31\ud800\udf30\ud800\udf3d (haban), Old English habban), from Proto-West Germanic *habbjan, from Proto-Germanic *habjan\u0105, from Proto-Indo-European *keh\u2082p- (\u201cto grasp\u201d). Cognate with Dutch hebben, English have, Danish have.\n

Verb[edit]

\n

haben (irregular, third-person singular simple present hat, past tense hatte, past participle gehabt, past subjunctive h\u00e4tte, auxiliary haben)\n

\n
  1. (auxiliary, with a past participle) to have (forms the perfect and past perfect tense)
    2. \n
  2. (transitive) to have; to own (to possess, have ownership of; to possess a certain characteristic)
    3. \n
  3. (transitive) to have; to hold (to contain within itself/oneself)\n
    Glaub und hab keine Angst.
    Believe and don't be afraid or Believe and have no fear.
    4. \n
  4. (transitive) to have, get (to obtain, acquire)
    5. \n
  5. (transitive) to get (to receive)
    6. \n
  6. (transitive) to have (to be scheduled to attend)
    7. \n
  7. (transitive) to have (to be afflicted with, suffer from)
    8. \n
  8. (transitive, of units of measure) to contain, be composed of, equal\n
    Ein Meter hat 100 Zentimeter.
    There are 100 centimetres in one metre.
    (literally, \u201cOne metre has 100 centimetres.\u201d)
    9. \n
  9. (impersonal, dialectal, with es) there be, there is, there are\n
    Es hat zwei B\u00fccher.
    There are two books.
    10. \n
  10. (reflexive, colloquial) to make a fuss\n
    Hab dich nicht so!
    Don't make such a fuss!
    11. \n
  11. (colloquial, with es and mit) to be occupied with, to like\n
    Ich hab's nich so mit Hunden.
    I'm not a great fan of dogs.
    (literally, \u201cI don't have it that much with dogs.\u201d)

Etymology[edit]

\n

From Proto-West Germanic *habbjan, from Proto-Germanic *habjan\u0105, whence also Old Saxon hebbian, Old English habban, Old Norse hafa, Gothic \ud800\udf37\ud800\udf30\ud800\udf31\ud800\udf30\ud800\udf3d (haban). Ultimately from Proto-Indo-European *keh\u2082p- (\u201cto grasp\u201d), whence also Latin capi\u014d.\n

Verb[edit]

hab\u0113n\n
